# Podcast Thinking Session — Chris Williamson × Kush
# Topic: Early Stage Startup Learnings
# Date: 2026-03-20
# Duration: ~15 minutes (async over Telegram)

---

**Chris:** Kush, man, welcome to Modern Wisdom. Good to have you here. So you're building Edith AI, you're in the trenches right now — early stage, Berlin-based, all of it. I love having founders on because there's this massive gap between what the startup advice industry tells you and what actually happens when you're in the arena. So let me start here — what's the one thing about early stage startups that you had to learn the hard way, that no amount of reading or podcasts could have prepared you for?

**Kush:** Hey Chris, thanks for having me, you've been doing some good work here on the pod, excited to be here. Yeah early stage startups are a messy place to be. I think there is a lot of stimulus and as a founder your only job is to focus on finding the direction which translates to revenue, especially now with all the AI acceleration, revenue is the only signal.

**Chris:** Revenue is the only signal — I love that. And I think that's a really important distinction because there's this whole ecosystem now, especially in AI, where you can fool yourself with other metrics. You can have thousands of users, incredible engagement, people saying "this is amazing" on Twitter, and yet nobody's actually paying you. And the stimulus piece is interesting because that's almost the founder's version of the attention crisis — a hundred possible directions, every one feels urgent. So when you say finding the direction that translates to revenue — how do you actually do that filtering in practice?

**Kush:** That's another advantage of AI — if you know what to do, the execution can be super fast. So if you have 10 ideas, just pick up an AI content pipeline and spam TikTok videos on all of them through separate accounts for a few days and see which resonate the most with the crowd.

**Chris:** So you're basically using AI as a rapid prototyping tool for market validation — not building the product first, but testing the narrative first. That's lean startup on steroids. But isn't there a risk that what resonates on TikTok isn't necessarily what people will pay for? Engagement and virality are not the same as willingness to open a wallet. How do you reconcile those two signals?

**Kush:** That's something that needs to be verified. But let me ask you this — would you rather build something that you think people will pay for but got no distribution on socials, or something which already went viral?

**Chris:** The viral one. Because distribution is the hard part now. Building is getting cheaper by the day. But getting attention? That's the scarce resource. Alex Hormozi said on the show — "you can have the best product in the world but if nobody knows about it, you have a hobby, not a business." But with AI making it so easy for everyone to do this — what's the actual moat for an early stage startup in 2026?

**Kush:** That's the reality. I think what we see on Twitter is a very small subset of the market. Just because someone got 100k views on a product launch doesn't make them the undisputed winner. The consumer market is big enough and you probably won't find the users on Twitter. So you can build and get distribution elsewhere — just need to make sure your LTV is greater than your CAC. On the moat part — things that compound will be the moat. Memory, history, data that makes it harder to switch. Intelligence from masses using your product. And speed — if you can build and sell faster than anyone, you have nothing to worry about.

**Chris:** The Twitter bubble observation is underrated. The actual market — the person in Ohio who needs that tool for their small business — they've never seen that tweet. There's an arbitrage in going where other founders aren't looking. But on compounding moats — OpenAI has that flywheel too, at a billion times more scale. How do you think about competing with incumbents who have the same logic but vastly more data?

**Kush:** Yeah, that's why going niche feels more important than ever. But the bigger labs are also building these use cases. An interesting thing is that's also giving a boom to dumb consumer apps — apps that would do 10-20k per month, and have multiple of those. That's what indie hackers are doing. But you won't see the next unicorn built like this. You'd need a step change of experience.

**Chris:** A step change of experience — say more about that. What does a step change actually look like? Can you give me a concrete example?

**Kush:** I'm not sure what that looks like right now — otherwise I would be building it. But to give you an example, OpenClaw was a step change.

**Chris:** OpenClaw — an open-source AI agent that runs on your own infrastructure, plugs into your messaging apps, has access to your files, calendar, email. What made that a step change rather than just another AI wrapper?

**Kush:** It's kind of the same moment developers felt when Cursor first came out. It allowed them to hook up their entire PC and just do everything with an agent that recursively tries to figure things out. The retry loop — OpenClaw figuring out and being resourceful to get stuff done — was one of the most important things. Because now you could actually get stuff done end to end. Not just give it a prompt and see what it comes up with. It unleashed a new experience of interacting with AI and getting work done.

**Chris:** The Cursor moment. Cursor didn't invent code completion — but it collapsed the gap between "AI gives you an answer" and "AI actually changes your codebase." From advisory to agentic. And the retry loop is key — tools don't retry, collaborators do. So you're building Edith AI, you've experienced what a step change feels like — what's the step change you want Edith to deliver?

**Kush:** With Edith, we were trying to deliver this exact step change that OpenClaw delivered in an open source manner. So I'm at a point where I'm figuring out what to build next. The most viable option seems to be using OpenClaw to implement solutions for SMBs and businesses — build an AI-driven agency. Focus on distribution and revenue, see what use cases come in, what data comes in, and form decisions from there. The market is big enough for people who don't know how to use OpenClaw but are already making money.

**Chris:** So the step change already happened, someone else built it, and now the question is: compete or ride the wave? The agency model is a classic picks-and-shovels play. But there's a tension — the builder identity wants the unicorn, the pragmatist wants to make money and survive. Where are you with that tension?

**Kush:** It definitely feels like the pragmatist approach. It's not the next big thing per se, but it would make money. Similar to the healthcare thing I'm exploring in UAE.

**Chris:** Healthcare in the UAE — what are you exploring there?

**Kush:** It's an opportunity I have right now. A bunch of pharmacies and clinics are at my disposal where I could implement a solution. The industry is archaic — we can AI-enable tools and build better systems for them, monetize per patient. Healthcare is a field where people are still going to eat medicines. So that's something I've been exploring.

**Chris:** Per patient monetization — strong model because it scales naturally. And the archaic industry point is where the biggest opportunities are. Everyone in Silicon Valley builds for developers and marketers. Nobody's building for the pharmacist in Dubai still doing inventory on a spreadsheet. But you've got three threads — Edith, the agency play, healthcare. Hormozi said "the enemy of the successful entrepreneur isn't bad ideas, it's too many good ones." How are you thinking about that?

**Kush:** Currently I'm thinking of going all in on the healthcare one and keeping building some random stuff on the side to see how it plays out. The healthcare angle seems pretty promising and might be worth doing for a few months.

**Chris:** Healthcare has something the other two don't — a customer who's already waiting. You skip "find product-market fit" and go straight to "can I deliver value." And the random stuff on the side is underrated — low-cost experiments while your main thing generates revenue. That's how you find the step change. One last question — zoom out five years. Is it easier or harder for founders to build something meaningful?

**Kush:** In the short term, consumer apps are gonna blow up. We'll have apps for every little use case that wasn't feasible to build 5 years back. Long term I'm still not sure what plays out. Maybe something on brain-computer interfaces with AI.

**Chris:** A Cambrian explosion of micro-apps. And BCI with AI long term — that's the real step change, when the interface disappears entirely. Listen Kush, I've really enjoyed this. What I'm taking away is something simple but hard to execute — in a world where everyone chases the next big thing, the founder who wins is disciplined enough to follow the revenue signal, stay close to real customers, and trust that the bigger insight emerges from doing the work. Healthcare first, experiments on the side, eyes open for the step change. Solid playbook. Thanks for being honest about where you're at — founders don't do that enough. Appreciate you coming on, man.

## Key Themes
1. **Revenue as the only signal** — vanity metrics vs. actual willingness to pay
2. **AI-powered market validation** — content-first testing before building product
3. **Distribution > product** — attention is the scarce resource, building is cheap
4. **Twitter ≠ the market** — tech bubble blinds founders to the real consumer market
5. **Compounding moats** — data, memory, switching costs + raw speed
6. **Step change vs. incremental** — advisory to agentic (Cursor/OpenClaw moment)
7. **Builder vs. pragmatist tension** — unicorn dreams vs. revenue reality
8. **Archaic industries = opportunity** — healthcare, pharmacies, underserved by tech
9. **Focus problem** — too many good ideas is the enemy
10. **Short-term: micro-app explosion / Long-term: BCI + AI**
